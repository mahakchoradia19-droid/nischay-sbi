Every cycle, the government sends money straight into people's accounts — for farmers, workers, students. It's already approved. For millions of people in rural India, it never lands, for one small, fixable reason. This is the system that notices, and fixes it, before the money turns around and goes home.

Meet Ramesh.

Ramesh is a farmer. Last week the government approved his ₹2,000 support payment — money he is entitled to, money that has already been sent. It never reached him.

Not fraud. Not a mistake he made. His bank account had quietly gone to sleep months ago from lack of use, and his ID verification had lapsed. So when the money came knocking, the door was shut. The payment turned around and went back to the government. Ramesh doesn't even know it happened. Next season, it will happen again.

There are millions of Rameshes. The bank's computers can see this coming. Today, nobody uses that to help. That is the entire gap.

Ramesh is one name in a morning's queue.

Before a payment cycle runs, the system lines up every account that's about to fail — what's wrong with each, and who can fix it. Most are fixable by voice in seconds. The rest are handed to a human, on purpose. This is the operational view: an agent triaging a cohort, not a single lucky story.

The questions you're going to ask.

Answered before you ask them — because pre-empting the hard question is the whole game.

Why hasn't SBI already done this, if it's so obvious?

Because the pieces sit in different rooms that don't talk. The team that reconciles bounced payments, the team that handles KYC, and the team that reaches customers are separate — and nobody owns "the gap" itself. Branches aren't even measured on rescued payments, so no one chases it. And the one genuinely missing piece — a cheap way to fix an account at the last mile, in the person's language, with no human at a counter — simply didn't exist until voice AI got good enough. The idea isn't new because we're clever; it's newly possible.

Is it even allowed to use people's data this way?

This is the most defensible possible use of it. We aren't mining data to sell anything — we're using it to deliver a citizen their own entitlement, the exact purpose the data was entrusted for. It's structured so the scheme/government side is the reason the outreach exists, with SBI as the executor of a delivery it is already mandated to perform. "Consent" and "use it only for the purpose it was given" aren't obstacles here — they're satisfied by the nature of what we do.

Why is this an AI project and not a database query?

The detection is basically a query — deliberately, so a regulator can audit it. The AI is concentrated in exactly one place: having a real, patient, multilingual conversation that fixes a broken account without a branch visit, and self-heals the small document mismatches that normally dead-end these flows. That's the thing a spreadsheet can't do and a call centre can't afford. We claim AI narrowly and precisely, right where the human problem is hardest.

Doesn't "fix your KYC by talking" break RBI's rules?

No — and we're precise about this: the conversation re-skins the mandatory steps, it doesn't remove them. The same verification RBI requires still happens — identity checked against documents, screening run, every step logged — the voice is just a friendlier front door to it. And the agent never moves money. It only unblocks the account; the credit itself arrives from the government through the existing payment rails. The reactivation gate is deterministic code: no reactivation without a reconciled identity, a clear screen, and the person's explicit confirmation. The talker can't overrule the gate — by design.

What happens when the call drops halfway? (It will.)

In rural India the interrupted session is the normal case — the network blinks, the shop gets busy, the phone dies. So the journey is remembered on the server, not in the page: every step Ramesh completes is saved as a stage, and when contact resumes — hours later, even from a different device — the follow-up points at the exact unfinished step: "you were almost done, one answer left." Never start over, never re-ask what's already answered. Try it in the demo: drop the call mid-confirmation and watch the system pick up precisely where he left off.

Isn't it risky to point AI at the people least able to catch its mistakes?

That's the most important question here, and the design answers it structurally rather than with promises. The agent's action space is tiny and closed — it can verify identity and reactivate an account; it cannot transfer money, sell a product, or change anything else. Every consequential decision runs through deterministic code, not the model's judgment. Anything ambiguous — an unclear reply, a genuine document conflict, low confidence — defaults to a human being, not to the AI pushing through. And the worst case of a wrong answer is a payment that stays exactly as blocked as it already was. We built the system so that the person who can least afford an AI mistake is the person the AI can least affect.

What's real today — and the ask

The whole loop — the noticing, the sorting by cause, the voice agent that reactivates an account, and an honest scorecard that reports its own misses — is built and running, on realistic sample data (we don't have SBI's real data). The logic and the voice are real; only the people are simulated. We'd rather show a real system on fake data than a fake system on real promises.

The ask: run this for real, in one limited area, for one payment cycle — with properly permissioned data — and measure it the honest way. Not just "how many did we rescue," but the number that actually matters: six months later, is the account still active, and did the next payment land on its own? Because the win isn't rescuing the money once. It's making sure Ramesh never misses it again.
